# Agentic AI Funnel Audit: Technical Business Plan and Go-to-Market Draft

This document is a working strategy draft for turning Agentic AI Funnel Audit from a demo application into a launchable software product. It is intentionally separate from the main project README so the product, commercial, and operating assumptions can evolve independently.

## Purpose

Agentic AI Funnel Audit is designed to help enterprise teams evaluate ideas, pilots, and change initiatives before funding or implementation begins. The software applies multiple evaluation lenses, produces an audit trail, and supports a clear gate decision that balances strategic value, delivery realism, governance, and safety.

The product thesis is simple: many organizations have more ideas than execution capacity, but most screening processes are still inconsistent, political, or poorly documented. This software creates a repeatable decision layer that reduces bias, improves traceability, and helps management allocate resources more confidently.

## Working assumptions

- The first commercial wedge is regulated or operationally complex enterprises rather than startups.
- The strongest near-term fit is with innovation, transformation, operations, architecture, and governance teams.
- The first version is a web-based management product, with mobile treated as a secondary interface for approvals and notifications.
- Buyers care more about decision quality, auditability, and prioritization discipline than about agent novelty alone.
- Early customers will expect configuration, onboarding support, and some advisory input before the product becomes more self-serve.

## Technical Business Plan

### Product definition

Agentic AI Funnel Audit is a decision-support platform for enterprise intake, triage, and gate governance. It combines:

- structured idea intake
- multi-agent scoring across operational, market, safety, and governance dimensions
- policy-aware pass or fail decisions
- explainable scorecards and rationale
- audit artifacts for leadership review

The platform is not intended to replace portfolio planning systems, GRC platforms, or project delivery tools. Its role is upstream: deciding which opportunities should progress, under what conditions, and with which risks explicitly understood.

### Problem being solved

Most organizations struggle with one or more of the following issues:

- idea evaluation depends too heavily on opinion or executive influence
- governance checks happen late, after momentum and cost are already committed
- data about risk, feasibility, and strategic fit is scattered across teams
- there is no common language for comparing unlike proposals
- leadership cannot easily explain why one initiative moved forward and another did not

This product addresses those gaps by creating a transparent gate process that turns subjective discussions into documented decisions.

### Target users

Primary users:

- Chief Technology and AI Officers
- transformation leaders
- innovation portfolio managers
- enterprise architects
- operations and governance leads

Secondary users:

- line-of-business sponsors
- PMO and strategy teams
- compliance and risk reviewers
- executive approvers

### Scope of the initial product

In scope for the initial commercial product:

- configurable intake workflow for proposed initiatives
- configurable scoring weights and approval thresholds
- multi-agent evaluation pipeline with clear rationale output
- dashboard views for review, override, and audit history
- exportable audit report artifacts
- API integration points for external systems and future telemetry enrichment

Out of scope for the initial product:

- full project portfolio management
- downstream project execution and sprint tracking
- full model lifecycle management for external AI workloads
- industry-specific claims or underwriting engines

### Strategic value proposition

The software helps management answer four questions earlier and more consistently:

1. Is this aligned to strategy?
2. Is this realistically feasible with current constraints?
3. Is the proposal safe and governable?
4. Should the organization invest now, later, or not at all?

That value is strongest where decisions are expensive, cross-functional, and regulated.

### Business outcomes targeted

Expected outcomes for customers include:

- faster screening of initiatives before detailed business-case work begins
- reduced funding of weak or non-executable proposals
- improved consistency across business units and review committees
- better audit readiness for technology and AI-related decisions
- clearer prioritization conversations between strategy, operations, and governance stakeholders

### Delivery model

The recommended product delivery model is phased:

1. Advisory-led pilot with one business function or innovation board.
2. Configured deployment across multiple decision domains.
3. Platform standardization with templates, policy packs, and integrations.
4. Expansion into enterprise-wide operating model support.

This phased model reduces adoption risk and allows the software to prove value on real decisions before broader rollout.

### Risks and mitigation

Key risks:

- stakeholders may view scoring as too rigid or too theoretical
- data quality may be too weak for richer evidence-based scoring
- teams may resist governance if they see it as a blocker rather than an enabler
- customers may compare the product to broader platforms with much larger feature sets

Mitigation approach:

- start with lightweight decision support rather than heavy process control
- allow policy thresholds and weights to be configurable
- emphasize explainability and override workflows
- position the product as a decision layer that complements existing systems

## Go-to-Market Strategy

### Market positioning

The clearest position is not "another AI tool". The product should be positioned as an enterprise decision governance and prioritization platform for AI, digital, and transformation initiatives.

Recommended positioning statement:

"Agentic AI Funnel Audit helps enterprise leaders decide which initiatives should move forward by combining strategic alignment, feasibility, governance, and safety into one auditable gate process."

### Ideal customer profile

Best-fit early customers:

- mid-market to large enterprises
- regulated industries such as insurance, financial services, healthcare, energy, or public sector
- organizations actively evaluating AI or transformation opportunities
- teams with fragmented intake or inconsistent governance practices
- leaders who need traceable justification for funding decisions

### Initial vertical wedge

The strongest initial wedge appears to be insurance and financial services because:

- decisions are risk-sensitive
- governance requirements are high
- transformation backlogs are large
- multiple teams compete for funding and attention
- leadership benefits from a defensible approval trail

This wedge can later expand into adjacent regulated sectors.

### Buyer and champion map

Economic buyer:

- CTAIO, CIO, COO, or transformation executive

Functional champion:

- innovation lead, architecture lead, PMO lead, or AI governance lead

Influencers:

- risk, compliance, operations, and business-unit sponsors

End users:

- reviewers, analysts, and approvers participating in intake and gate workflows

### Sales motion

Recommended early sales motion:

1. Lead with an executive problem statement: too many initiatives, weak prioritization discipline, and poor traceability.
2. Run a discovery workshop using the customer's current decision process.
3. Pilot the software on a limited number of real proposals.
4. Show measurable improvements in consistency, speed, and approval quality.
5. Convert the pilot into an annual platform agreement with onboarding and support.

This is more likely to succeed as a consultative B2B motion than as a pure product-led motion in the first stage.

### Packaging approach

Suggested packaging model:

- Foundation: core intake, scoring, gate decisioning, and audit artifacts
- Professional: configurable policy packs, dashboards, exports, and workflow support
- Enterprise: SSO, integrations, advanced governance controls, and tailored operating model support

An implementation or advisory package should be available for pilot customers.

### Go-to-market channels

Recommended channels:

- founder-led outbound to innovation and transformation leaders
- LinkedIn thought leadership around AI governance and prioritization discipline
- executive briefings and small workshops
- partnerships with advisory firms or boutique transformation consultancies
- selected industry demos focused on regulated sectors

### Messaging priorities

Message hierarchy:

- first: improve decision quality and funding discipline
- second: create an auditable governance trail
- third: accelerate responsible AI and transformation screening
- fourth: use multi-agent evaluation to reduce blind spots

The product should not be sold primarily on novelty. It should be sold on management confidence, clarity, and portfolio discipline.

## Commercial assumptions

### Revenue model hypothesis

The most credible early revenue model is B2B SaaS with a services-assisted implementation layer.

Potential revenue components:

- annual platform subscription
- user or reviewer seat bands
- implementation and configuration fee
- optional advisory or policy design package
- optional premium integration and support tier

### Pricing hypothesis

Illustrative pricing direction for early enterprise deals:

- pilot package: fixed-fee paid discovery and limited deployment
- annual subscription: based on organization size, review volume, and governance complexity
- enterprise expansion: additional fees for integrations, SSO, and bespoke policy frameworks

The exact price point should be validated through early customer interviews and pilot conversion rates rather than fixed too early.

### Commercial assumptions to validate

- Sales cycles may range from 8 to 20 weeks depending on governance sensitivity.
- One strong champion is usually required to move from demo to pilot.
- Early deals will likely require services support even if the software itself is productized.
- The strongest proof point is not model sophistication; it is improved decision consistency and reduced wasted effort.
- Renewal potential increases when the product becomes embedded in a standing intake or governance forum.

### Cost assumptions

Major cost areas likely include:

- product engineering and maintenance
- cloud hosting and storage
- security and compliance controls
- sales and customer discovery time
- onboarding and customer success support
- optional model API usage where external models are enabled

### Core commercial metrics

Metrics worth tracking from the first live customers:

- pilot-to-subscription conversion rate
- average sales cycle length
- average annual contract value
- number of reviews processed per customer per quarter
- percentage of gated decisions with explicit rationale accepted by stakeholders
- customer expansion from one function to multiple functions
- retention and renewal rate

## Roadmap and next steps

### Product roadmap

#### Phase 1: Prove the wedge

Time horizon: 0 to 3 months

- tighten the web product for demo and pilot use
- finalize core scorecard logic and policy configuration
- improve reporting and artifact export quality
- document one to two reference use cases in regulated industries
- prepare pilot onboarding material and reviewer guidance

#### Phase 2: Convert demos into pilots

Time horizon: 3 to 6 months

- onboard first pilot customers or internal design partners
- capture decision outcomes and reviewer feedback
- add administrator controls and workflow refinement
- strengthen dashboard views for leadership review
- validate pricing and packaging assumptions

#### Phase 3: Productize enterprise adoption

Time horizon: 6 to 12 months

- add SSO and enterprise access controls
- add integration connectors for intake sources and governance systems
- introduce reusable policy templates by industry or use case
- improve analytics around portfolio patterns and gate outcomes
- formalize implementation and customer success playbooks

#### Phase 4: Scale and differentiate

Time horizon: 12 to 18 months

- broaden from AI initiative screening into wider transformation decision governance
- add benchmark reporting across categories of proposals
- deepen explainability and evidence capture
- support mobile-friendly approvals and executive notifications
- evaluate ecosystem partnerships and regional expansion

### Immediate next steps

Recommended next actions for the repo and product narrative:

1. Turn this document into a living strategy draft updated after each customer conversation.
2. Define one ideal-customer-profile story for insurance and one cross-industry story for regulated enterprises.
3. Create a simple pilot offer with scope, timeline, deliverables, and pricing hypothesis.
4. Build a short demo script that shows intake, scoring, gate decision, override, and audit export.
5. Capture a comparison view against adjacent tools such as portfolio management, GRC, and AI governance platforms.

## Summary

The software has credible potential as a B2B product if it is positioned as a decision governance layer rather than as a generic AI app. Its best path is to start with a focused enterprise wedge, prove measurable value through pilot decisions, and then expand through configurable policy-driven workflows, auditability, and integration into existing management processes.

This document should be treated as a working draft for product strategy, commercial shaping, and future investor, buyer, or internal planning discussions.
